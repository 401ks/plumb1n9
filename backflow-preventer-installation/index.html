Backflow Preventer Installation – Protect Your Drinking Water from Contamination

Your plumbing system is designed to move clean water in one direction—into your home or business.

However, under certain conditions, water can unexpectedly reverse direction. This is known as backflow, and it can allow contaminated water from irrigation systems, boilers, industrial equipment, or other sources to enter your clean drinking water supply.

A backflow preventer is a safety device designed to stop this from happening. Proper installation helps protect your property, your family's health, and in some cases the wider public water supply.

What Is Backflow?

Backflow occurs when water flows in the opposite direction from normal. Instead of clean water moving from the municipal supply into your property, contaminated water may be pulled or pushed backward into the drinking water system.

Back-Siphonage

A sudden drop in water pressure can create a vacuum that pulls water backward through connected plumbing. May occur during water main repairs, fire hydrant use, major pipe bursts, or high water demand.

Backpressure

If pressure inside a property's plumbing system becomes higher than the incoming water supply, contaminated water can be forced back into the clean water line. More common with booster pumps, boilers, commercial equipment, irrigation systems, and industrial processes.

Why Is a Backflow Preventer Important?

Without a properly functioning backflow preventer, contaminants may enter the potable water supply. Potential contaminants include fertilizers, pesticides, cleaning chemicals, bacteria, wastewater, industrial fluids, and stagnant irrigation water. While backflow incidents are uncommon, they can occur without obvious warning, making prevention far more effective than dealing with contamination after it happens.

Where Are Backflow Preventers Installed?

  • Irrigation and sprinkler systems
  • Commercial buildings
  • Medical facilities
  • Restaurants
  • Apartment buildings
  • Fire sprinkler systems
  • Water purification equipment
  • Boiler systems
  • Main water supply connections

Not every property requires the same type of device. The correct solution depends on the level of contamination risk and applicable plumbing standards.

Types of Backflow Prevention Devices

  • Double Check Valve Assembly (DCVA) – Suitable for many low to medium hazard applications where the contamination risk is relatively low.
  • Pressure Vacuum Breaker (PVB) – Frequently installed on landscape irrigation systems to protect against back-siphonage.
  • Reduced Pressure Zone (RPZ) Assembly – Provides one of the highest levels of protection and is commonly used where there is a greater risk of contamination, such as commercial or industrial properties.

Choosing the correct device depends on your property's plumbing layout, water source, and regulatory requirements.

Signs Your Property May Need a Backflow Preventer

  • An underground sprinkler system
  • A private well connected to other water systems
  • A boiler or hydronic heating system
  • Commercial kitchen equipment
  • Chemical injection systems
  • Industrial machinery
  • Fire suppression systems

Many local authorities also require backflow prevention for certain plumbing installations to help protect the public water network.

Professional Installation Matters

Installing a backflow preventer involves more than fitting a valve into a pipe. The device must be correctly sized, match the hazard level, be installed in the proper orientation, be accessible for inspection and maintenance, and meet applicable plumbing regulations. Improper installation may reduce the effectiveness of the device and could result in failed inspections or non-compliance with local requirements.

Maintenance and Testing

Like any mechanical component, backflow preventers should be inspected and maintained. Depending on local regulations and the type of device installed, periodic testing may also be required to confirm that the internal valves continue to operate correctly. Regular inspections help identify worn seals, damaged check valves, and other issues before they affect water safety.

Protect Your Water Supply

Clean drinking water is something most people take for granted—until a problem occurs. A professionally installed backflow preventer adds an important layer of protection by helping prevent contaminated water from entering your plumbing system.
